## Goals of Nutrition Support
*   Promote positive clinical outcomes of illness for the patient
*   Improve quality of life
*   **The optimal route of nutrition support:** Gastrointestinal (GI) tract

## Effects of Malnutrition
*   Poor wound healing
*   Infection
*   Increased length of hospital stays
*   Increased mortality

## Nutrition Support: Delivery Methods

| Delivery Method | Description |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Enteral Nutrition (EN)** | For patients that cannot meet nutritional goals through normal diet. **GI tract is working!** |
| **Parenteral Nutrition (PN)** | For patients that cannot eat or maintain fluid/nutrition status via GI tract. |

### Enteral vs. Parenteral Nutrition Comparison

| Feature | Enteral Nutrition (EN) | Parenteral Nutrition (PN) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Advantages** | - Generally preferred<br>- Supports gut function<br>- Hyperglycemia less likely | - Independent of GI tract |
| **Disadvantages** | - Diarrhea<br>- Aspiration<br>- Requires GI tract functionality | - Requires IV access (Infection, Thrombosis)<br>- Hyperglycemia and other metabolic/electrolyte abnormalities<br>- Fluid overload |

## Patient Assessment & Nutritional Requirements

### Body Weight (ABW vs IBW)
*   **Actual Body Weight (ABW)**
*   **Ideal Body Weight (IBW):** Population reference standard so we can compare ABW.
    *   **IBW in MEN** = 50 kg + (2.3 × inches > 5ft)
    *   **IBW in WOMEN** = 45.5 kg + (2.3 × inches > 5ft)
*   **Weight Classifications:**
    *   Overweight is defined as > 120% of IBW
    *   Obesity is defined as > 150% of IBW
    *   Morbid obesity is defined as > 200% of IBW

### Other Anthropometric Measures
*   **Body Mass Index (BMI):** Reported as kg/m²
    *   NIH classifies 18.5-24.9 kg/m² as a healthy weight
    *   BMI ≥ 25 (overweight), BMI ≥ 30 (obese)
*   **Waist Circumference (WC):**
    *   Measurement of abdominal (visceral) fat. Distance around the waist above iliac crest after exhaling.
    *   Men and women with WC > 40” and 35” respectively have increased risk of weight-related complications (diabetes).
*   **Waist to Hip Ratio:**
    *   Ratio > 0.9 in men and > 0.85 in women is correlated with negative health consequences.
    *   Measures “abdominal obesity”, which is independently correlated with development of metabolic syndrome.
*   **Skinfold Thickness:**
    *   Measure of tricep skinfold thickness (using calipers).
    *   Estimates body’s subcutaneous fat; changes to subcutaneous fat reflects changes to total body fat.
*   **Bioelectrical Impedance (BIA):**
    *   Quick, non-invasive measure that utilizes electrical current to determine body composition.
    *   Water/electrolyte-rich tissues (blood and muscle) are highly conductive compared with fat and bone.
    *   Clinical use is limited due to impact of hydration status of patients.

### Estimating Caloric Needs

#### Weight-Based Estimation

| Patient Condition | Caloric Requirement |
| :--- | :--- |
| Healthy, normal nutritional status, minimal illness | 20-25 kcals/kg/day |
| Illness, metabolic stress, BMI < 30 kg/m² | 25-30 kcals/kg/day |
| Illness, metabolic stress, BMI ≥ 30 kg/m² | 11-14 kcals/kg/day |
| Major burn injury | 30 kcals/kg/day |
| **Class Standard** | **We will use 25 kcal/kg** |

#### Harris-Benedict Equation (Basal Energy Expenditure - BEE)
*   **For men:** 66 + (13.75 × W [kg]) + (5 × H [cm]) - (6.8 × A [yrs])
*   **For women:** 655 + (9.6 × W [kg]) + (1.8 × H [cm]) - (4.7 × A [yrs])
*   **Total Energy Requirement:** [BEE] × [stress factor] or [activity factor]
    *   Stress factor based upon amount of physiologic stress due to illness.
    *   Activity factor based on level of physical activity.
    *   **Class Standard:** We will use a Stress Factor (SF) of 1.3.

### Daily Fluid Requirements
*   **Normal daily requirement:** 30 mL/kg of body mass
    *   From all sources (IV infusions, per tube, or oral intake).
    *   Can be supplemented outside of nutrition support.
*   **Patients that may require MORE fluid:**
    *   Vomiting/diarrhea
    *   Fever
    *   Elevated RR rates
    *   Increased metabolism
*   **Patients that may require LESS fluid:**
    *   Heart failure
    *   Kidney failure

## Enteral Nutrition (EN)

### Benefits of EN (Tube Feeding)
*   Maintains GI function
*   More physiologic
*   Stimulates bile flow
*   Fewer metabolic complications (Less risk of hypoglycemia, less risk of electrolyte abnormalities)
*   Reduced risk of infection

### Enteral Formulas
Content of formulas vary in macronutrient complexity (i.e., how easily it breaks down to facilitate absorption).

#### Standard Formula (Polymeric)
*   Most provide **1 kcal/mL**.
*   Supplies patient with all the nutritional requirements.
*   **Carbohydrates:** Majority of kcals come from carbohydrates. Contains mostly complex carbs, as well as simple sugars and fiber.
*   **Protein:** Contains essential and non-essential AA.
*   **Fat:** Commonly in the form of soy or corn oil.
*   **Indication:** Most common. For patients with fully functional GI tract (can digest and absorb nutrients). Contains intact proteins and mix of carbohydrate sources.

#### Oligomeric Formula
*   “Predigested” or hydrolyzed.
*   **Indication:** For patients with compromised digestive or absorptive functions.
*   Contain partially broken down carbohydrates and proteins.

#### Specialty Formulas

| Formula Type | Indication / Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- |
| **High Protein** | Indicated for critically ill, surgical wound (healing) |
| **High Caloric Density** | Indicated for those with high caloric needs; fluid restricted. **Provides up to 2 kcals/mL** |
| **Elemental/Peptide Based** | Indicated for those with impaired digestion or absorption (short bowel); pancreatitis |
| **Disease Specific – Renal** | Lower in electrolytes, calorie dense |
| **Disease Specific – Liver** | Increase branched chain AA |
| **Disease Specific – Pulm** | High in fat; indicated for patients with lung injury or respiratory distress syndrome |
| **Disease Specific – Diabetes** | High fat, low carbohydrates |

### EN Access: Short Term vs Long Term
Based on anticipated duration of need.

*   **Short Term Access:** Easier, less invasive, less costly.
    *   **Nasogastric (NG):** *Most common* (nose → stomach). Typically have smallest bore size. Appropriate for feedings and drug administration. Easily clogged.
    *   **Nasoduodenal (ND), Nasojejunal (NJ)**
    *   **Orogastric (OG):** Reserved for those with inaccessible nasopharyngeal area.
    *   *Note:* Feeding to stomach is preferred (physiologic), but there is an increased risk of aspiration/pneumonia.
*   **Long Term Access:** Indicated when EN is expected to last > 4-6 weeks.
    *   **Gastrostomy (PEG):** *Most common* or **Jejunostomy (J tube)**.
    *   Large bore, less likely to clog.
    *   More comfortable for patient (no nasal irritation).

### EN Administration Options
1.  **Continuous:**
    *   Constant rate throughout the day (over 24 hours).
    *   Preferred when initiating EN. Hospitalized patients almost always have continuous feeds.
    *   May be administered gastric or post-pyloric.
    *   Larger volumes may be associated with aspiration, diarrhea, and cramping.
    *   Less likely to cause abdominal distention, N/V/D, cramping.
2.  **Cyclic:**
    *   Given over 12 – 18 hours, typically overnight.
    *   Helpful when trying to re-initiate oral nutrition (patients will complain of “feeling full” or have lack of appetite when on continuous EN).
    *   May provide greater convenience and mobility for appropriate patients.
    *   May be administered gastric or post-pyloric.
3.  **Bolus:**
    *   Delivery over 5 to 10 minutes, most physiologic.
    *   Only equipment needed: syringe.
    *   Instillation volume of 240 to 500 mL four to six times daily.
    *   May result in cramping, nausea, vomiting, aspiration, and diarrhea.
    *   Administered **gastric only**.
    *   *Contraindication:* Should be avoided in patients with delayed gastric emptying and in patients who are at high risk of aspiration.
4.  **Intermittent:**
    *   Delivery over 20 to 60 minutes.
    *   Patient intolerant to bolus feedings due to cramping, N/V/D, or aspiration.
    *   Administered **gastric only**.
    *   More commonly used in children.

### EN and Medications
Considerations for use of feeding tube to administer medications:
*   Is the drug enteric coated? Extended or sustained release?
*   Can it be crushed?
*   Does drug require absorption in stomach (low pH)?
*   Does food affect absorption?

### EN Complications
*   **Metabolic complications:** Associated with PN may also occur (e.g., hyperglycemia, hepatic dysfunction, etc.), but much lower incidence with EN.
*   **GI complications:** Cramping, N/V, distention, aspiration, diarrhea, constipation.
    *   Tolerance of formula based on gastric residual volume (GRV).
    *   High residuals caused by: Delayed gastric emptying, Drugs (narcotics, anticholinergics).
*   **Aspiration (Major Concern):**
    *   AVOID high residuals by:
        *   Decrease rate.
        *   Starting prokinetic agent (**metoclopramide, erythromycin**).
        *   Start PPI or H2RA to decrease gastric secretions.
        *   Feed into small bowel (post-pyloric).

## Parenteral Nutrition (PN)

### PN Fluid Administration
*   Usually can give required nutrients in about 1500-2000 mL for adult patients.
*   In fluid-restricted patients: consider reducing PN volume to 1000 mL. Limits the amount of amino acid or dextrose solution that can be given.

### Determining Macronutrient Content (Step-by-Step)
1. Determine total calories needed (2 methods: weight-based or Harris-Benedict).
2. Determine protein requirement (*normally 1-1.2 g/kg*).
3. Determine how many non-protein calories are required:
   * 15-35% of kcals provided as IVFE.
   * Remaining calories provided as dextrose.

### PN Macronutrients (Major Sources of Energy)

#### 1. Protein / Amino Acids (AA)
*   Needed for physiologic function, maintaining muscle mass, tissue repair.
*   Need is dependent on level of metabolic stress (e.g., hospitalized > surgical > burn/trauma).
*   Dietary nitrogen intake. Too much protein: uremia, azotemia.
*   Nutrition support should provide approximately **1-1.2 g/kg of protein per day**.
*   **1 gram of amino acids provides 4 kcals.**
*   **Commercially available AA solutions:**
    *   Available in 10%, 15%, and 20% solutions. Contain all 9 essential AA.
    *   Variable amounts of the conditionally essential and non-essential AA.
    *   AA solutions improve calcium and phosphate solubility.
*   **Modified AA solutions:**
    *   *Liver failure:* contain more branched chain and less aromatic AA.
    *   *Renal failure:* decreased urea containing AA, increased concentrations of tyrosine (becomes essential in RF).

#### 2. Carbohydrates / Dextrose
*   Carbohydrates are needed for physiologic metabolic functioning, primary source of cellular energy.
*   Too much carbs: hyperglycemia, metabolic acidosis.
*   About **40-50% of calories** in a PN formula should be given as carbohydrates.
*   Typically given as **70% dextrose** solution.
*   **1 gram of dextrose provides 3.4 kcals.** *(Note: dietary carbohydrates provide 4 kcals/gram)*.

#### 3. Fats / Lipids (IVLE or IVFE)
*   In PN naïve patients, fats are initiated after dextrose/AA solution is tolerated.
*   Often **15-35% of calories** from lipids.
*   Used to supplement calories in PN. Fat is necessary for production of hormones. Generally, 3% of calories from fat is required for homeostasis.
*   **Result of deficiency:** EFAD (essential fatty acid deficiency). Linoleic and linolenic are essential for cellular integrity, immune function, and wound healing.
*   **Too much fat:** hypertriglyceridemia, immune suppression.
*   **Intravenous lipid emulsion for compounding:**
    *   Most contain soybean oil or a mix of soybean/safflower oil (Allergy concerns).
    *   Available concentrations: 10%, 20%, or 30%.
    *   **10% IVFE provides 1.1 kcals/mL**
    *   **20% IVFE provides 2 kcals/mL**
    *   **30% IVFE provides 3 kcals/mL**
    *   *(Note: dietary fats provide 9 kcals/gram)*.

#### 1. Vitamins

| :--- | :--- |

| Niacin (B3) | 40 mg |

| Vitamin E | 10 IU |
| Vitamin K | 150 mcg |

#### 2. Trace Elements
*   A standard trace element solution is given daily. Typically 3 mL are added to formula.
*   **Contains:** zinc, copper, manganese, chromium, selenium, molybdenum, iodine.
*   **Considerations:** Patients may accumulate/retain copper and manganese when severe biliary obstruction exists (i.e., total bilirubin > 6).

#### 3. Electrolytes
*   **Includes:** Cations (Na, K, Ca, Mg) and Anions (Cl, Acetate, Phosphate).
*   Given to maintain normal serum concentrations. Routine need for electrolytes are cellular function.
*   Decrease/remove based on patient-specific lab parameters. As a rule, should not be used as a method to correct electrolyte abnormalities.
*   **Acid base (acetate and chloride concentrations):**
    *   More chloride can help in alkalosis.
    *   Adding acetate may be useful in patients with acidosis.
*   **Potassium:**
    *   Requirements may be lower in patients with renal failure.
    *   Requirements may be higher in patients who have depleted potassium due to malnutrition.
*   **Sodium:**
    *   Requirements may be lower in patients with heart failure.
    *   Requirements may be higher in patients with sodium wasting disorders (e.g., GI disorders).
*   **Calcium / Phosphate Solubility:**
    *   May be adjusted in patients with underlying renal disease.
    *   Phosphate requirements may be higher in patients with malnutrition or high losses from GI output, etc.
    *   Very high amounts of Ca and PO4 can precipitate out of solution if concentrations are high.
    *   **Rule of 45:** mEq Ca/L + PO4 mMol/L ≤ 45 mEq/L
    *   **Rule of 200:** mEq Ca/L × PO4 mMol/L ≤ 200 mEq/L
    *   If incompatible, consider electrolyte replacement outside of PN.

### PN Administration: Drug Additives
*   There are some medications may be added directly into the PN. Reduces burden of administration of other IV medications.
*   **What drugs can be added safely?**
    *   Vitamins (folate, thiamine)
    *   Heparin
    *   Iron Dextran
    *   H2RA (famotidine, ranitidine)
    *   Insulin Regular
*   **What drugs can be administered together: is it compatible?**
    *   Must check drug references: Trissel’s Handbook of Injectable Drugs (gold standard) or King Guide to Intravenous Admixtures. Both can be found in Micromedex and LexiComp.

### Administration of Parenteral Nutrition
There are 3 considerations for administration:
1.  2-in-1 formula OR 3-in-1 formula
2.  Central OR Peripheral
3.  Continuous OR Cyclic

#### Central vs Peripheral
*   Peripheral parenteral nutrition (PPN) is preferred route.
*   **Considerations:** Duration of therapy, Line placement/access, Patient’s nutritional requirements, Osmolarity.
*   **Osmolarity Rule:** If **≥ 900 mOsm/L**, MUST administer centrally.

#### Continuous vs Cyclic
*   **Continuous:** Runs over 24 hours.
    *   Better tolerated, stable blood glucose levels. More common in hospitalized.
    *   *Disadvantages:* risk of hepatotoxicity, challenging in patients with multiple IV meds.
*   **Cyclic:** Runs over 12 or 18 hours.
    *   Allows for greater mobility. More common in patients on TPN at home.
    *   *Disadvantages:* requires a stable regimen, may cause challenges in maintain fluid balance and glucose levels.

### PN Complications (3 Types)

#### 1. Mechanical
*   Pump set failure.
*   Thrombus formation or fibrin deposition at catheter tip.
*   **AVOID by:** administering small amounts of anticoagulants.

#### 2. Infectious
*   Catheter is a potential portal of entry (Fungal or bacterial).
*   Result in infections leading to sepsis.
*   Catheter-related blood stream infections in critically ill patients receiving PN is high at about 15-25%.
*   **AVOID by:** dedicating the catheter for PN administration only and changing the line when infection is present.

#### 3. Metabolic
*   **Hyperglycemia/hypoglycemia**
*   **Metabolic acidosis:** from excessive CO2 production.
    *   **AVOID by:** decreasing dextrose content (more calories as fat).
*   **Hypertriglyceridemia:**
    *   **AVOID by:** decreasing fat content.
*   **Hepatic failure:**
    *   **AVOID by:** decreasing macronutrients or changing to cyclic administration.
*   **Refeeding Syndrome:**
    *   Highest risk in severely malnourished patients.
    *   **AVOID by:** correcting electrolyte abnormalities and by slow initiation.

## Clinical Case Study: Patient "MR"

### Patient Presentation
MR is a 78-year-old man brought to the emergency department, who is unable to speak. He has left-sided facial palsy and left-sided weakness in both upper and lower extremities and is admitted to the hospital with acute ischemic stroke. After a speech and swallow evaluation, he is found to be at high risk of aspiration. Speech therapist reports that patient is unable to swallow.

### Physical Exam & Vitals
*   **Gen:** appears anxious and uncomfortable due to abdominal pain
*   **Height:** 5’9” (69 inches) = 175.26 cm
*   **Weight:** 165 lbs = 75 kg
*   **IBW:** 70.7 kg
*   **Vitals:** BP = 136/72, HR = 72, RR = 15, Tmax = 98.2

### Laboratory Values
*   **Na:** 142
*   **Cl:** 100
*   **BUN:** 10
*   **K:** 4.1
*   **CO2:** 25
*   **SCr:** 1.1
*   **Glucose:** 91
*   **Ca:** 9.4 (Normal: 8.5-10)
*   **Mg:** 2.1 (Normal: 1.8-2.5)
*   **PO4:** 3.4 (Normal: 2.6-4.5)
*   **Albumin:** 3.6 (Normal: 3.5-5)
*   **Pre-albumin:** 15 (Normal: 19.5-35.8)

### Initial Plan: Enteral Feeding
*   **Nutritional Requirement:** 1800 kcals/day
*   **Fluid Requirement:** ~2200 mL/day
*   **Option 1:** Start Nutren 1.0 (provides 1 kcal/mL).
*   **Option 2:** Start Jevity 1.2 (provides 1.2 kcals/mL).

### Revised Plan: Parenteral Nutrition
MR is not tolerating feedings due to increased residuals. Team is concerned about aspiration and a decision is made to start parenteral nutrition.

**TPN Order States:**
*   Start 1.8 liter (1800 mL), 1800 calorie TPN containing:
    *   1.2 g/kg as protein
    *   30% kcals as fat

**Pharmacy Stock Solutions Available:**
*   Aminosyn 15%
*   IVLE 20%
*   Dextrose 70%
