Enteral Nutrition (EN) Clinical Data Extraction

Key Concepts

  - The gastrointestinal (GI) tract defends the host from toxins and antigens
    via immunologic and nonimmunologic mechanisms (gut barrier function).
  - EN is preferred over parenteral nutrition (PN) due to lower risk of
    metabolic and infectious complications, lower cost, and less invasiveness.
  - Candidates for EN: Sufficiently functioning GI tract to allow adequate
    nutrient absorption, unable or unwilling to eat, and safe enteral access
    obtainable.
  - Critically ill patients benefit from early initiation of EN. Trophic feeds
    (low rates) are acceptable for the first week in most ICU patients. Severely
    malnourished patients should be advanced to goal as quickly as tolerated.
  - Most common route for short- and long-term EN access is directly into the
    stomach.
  - Delivery methods: Continuous via infusion pump, intermittent via pump or
    gravity drip, or bolus via gravity or syringe.
  - Patients unable to tolerate gastric delivery (impaired gastric motility)
    require duodenal or jejunal feeding. Small bowel feeding requires continuous
    delivery via infusion pump to enhance tolerance.
  - Standard polymeric formulation is appropriate for most adults.
  - EN-related diarrhea causes: Too rapid delivery/advancement, formula
    composition intolerance, formula contamination.
  - Medications that should not be crushed for tube administration:
    Enteric-coated, sustained-release capsules/tablets, sublingual, or buccal
    tablets.
  - EN coadministration can alter medication bioavailability/pharmacologic
    effects. Known interactions: Phenytoin, warfarin, levothyroxine, select
    antimicrobials, antacids, proton-pump inhibitors.

Patient Care Process for the Use of Enteral Nutrition

Collect

  - Patient characteristics (age, sex)
  - Patient history (medical, surgical, family, social/alcohol)
  - Nutrition history (dietary, weight, intolerance, prior EN/PN)
  - Current medications and nutritional supplements
  - Current diet and nutrition support regimen
  - Procedures related to enteral access placement
  - Objective data: Height, weight, BMI, fluid balance (I/O), labs
    (electrolytes, SCr, BUN, glucose), diagnostic tests (abdominal imaging,
    gastric emptying, swallow study)

Assess

  - Appropriate time to initiate EN
  - Presence of altered GI anatomy/function (surgeries, delayed emptying,
    pancreatic insufficiency)
  - Nutritional status and refeeding syndrome risk (unintentional weight loss,
    prolonged poor intake, BMI <18 kg/m², fat/muscle wasting)
  - Nutrition requirements (goal protein, calories, fluid, micronutrients)
  - Appropriate enteral access

Plan

  - EN regimen: Specific formula name, administration method (continuous,
    bolus), route (NG, j-tube), initiation/advancement guidelines.
  - Monitoring parameters: Efficacy (weight, intake), GI tolerance (stool
    output, nausea, vomiting, distension), metabolic complications
    (electrolytes, SCr, BUN, glucose, LFTs).

Implement

  - Initiate EN when oral intake is insufficient/impossible, GI tract is
    functional, and access is safe.
  - Patient education for home EN.

Follow-up: Monitor and Evaluate

  - Determine if nutrition goals are met.
  - Adjust regimen if goals unmet; consider PN if adjustments fail or
    intolerance develops.
  - Transition off EN when needs are safely met by oral diet.
  - Monitor adverse medication reactions and GI intolerance.

Gastrointestinal Tract Physiology

Digestion and Absorption

  - Carbohydrates: 4 kcal/g (17 kJ/g).
  - Protein: 4 kcal/g (17 kJ/g).
  - Fat: 9 kcal/g (38 kJ/g).

Gut Host Defense Mechanisms

  - Gut barrier prevents systemic spread of intraluminal bacteria and
    endotoxins.
  - Stomach hydrochloric acid kills ingested bacteria.
  - Mucus layer coats intestinal epithelium, favors anaerobic bacteria (prevents
    pathogen colonization).
  - Small bowel peristalsis prevents bacterial stasis/overgrowth.
  - Gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) regulates local immune response;
    secretes specific immunoglobulins.
  - Liver Kupffer cells clear portal blood of gut-derived bacteria/endotoxins.

Indications for Enteral Nutrition

  - Criteria: Insufficient oral intake, functional GI tract, safe EAD placement.
  - Contraindications: Bowel ischemia, necrotizing enterocolitis, mechanical
    obstruction in distal bowel.
  - Long-term EAD Contraindications: Active peritonitis, uncorrectable
    coagulopathy.
  - Conditions challenging EN use: Severe diarrhea, protracted vomiting, enteric
    fistulas, severe GI hemorrhage, hemodynamic instability, intestinal
    dysmotility.

Benefits of Enteral Nutrition

  - EN vs PN: EN supports functional gut integrity, stimulates bile flow,
    maintains mucosal villous height, supports IgA-producing immunocytes (GALT).
    Reduces risk of cholestasis, gallbladder sludge, and gallstones compared to
    PN. Less costly than PN.
  - Timing of Initiation:
      - Critically ill: Initiate within 24-48 hours of ICU admission (decreases
        disease severity, infectious complications, hospital LOS, mortality).
      - Hemodynamically unstable: Delay EN until fluid resuscitation is complete
        and vasopressors are withdrawn or at low, stable doses.
      - Mild-to-moderately stressed, well-nourished adults: Delay EN for 5-7
        days if oral intake is inadequate.

  - Short-Term Access: < 4 to 6 weeks. Nasogastric (NG), orogastric (OG),
    nasojejunal (NJ), orojejunal (OJ).
  - Long-Term Access: > 4 to 6 weeks. Gastrostomy (PEG), Jejunostomy,
    Gastrojejunal (GJ) tube.
      - Gastrostomy tubes: 12F-28F; 0.8-5 cm shaft lengths. Routine replacement
        every 3-6 months.
      - GJ tubes: Replaced every 3-6 months to prevent occlusion.

Administration Methods

  - Continuous: Target rates 50-125 mL/hr. Method of choice for hospitalized
    patients and mandatory for small intestine delivery.
  - Cyclic: Continuous rate over a portion of the day (e.g., nocturnal).
  - Bolus: 240-500 mL over 5-10 minutes, 4-6 times daily via syringe. Avoid in
    duodenal/jejunal access, delayed gastric emptying, or high aspiration risk.
  - Intermittent: Administered over 20-60 minutes every 4-6 hours.

Initiation and Advancement Protocol

  - Continuous EN: Start at 20-50 mL/hr, advance by 10-25 mL/hr every 4-8 hours
    until goal.
  - Intermittent EN: Start with 120 mL every 4 hours, advance by 30-60 mL
    every 8-12 hours.
  - Critically Ill: Permissive underfeeding (50%-80% of goal) or trophic EN
    (10-20 mL/hr) acceptable for short ICU stays. High nutrition risk patients
    should advance to goal over 24-48 hours.
  - Volume-Based Feeding: Target daily volume (max 150 mL/hr gastric, 125 mL/hr
    small bowel). Start at 20-25 mL/hr on day 1, initiate volume-based protocols
    on day 2.

Water Flushes

  - Flush with minimum 30 mL every 4 hours for continuous feedings.
  - Flush immediately before and after bolus/intermittent feedings and
    medication administration.
  - Purified water preferred for immunocompromised or critically ill patients.

  - Specific Dosing/Parameters:
      - Hypocaloric, high-protein EN for obesity: 2-2.5 g/kg ideal body
        weight/day.
      - Propofol lipid vehicle: 1.1 kcal/mL (4.6 kJ/mL).
      - Linoleic acid requirement: 1% to 3% of total daily calories.
      - Arginine concentration in immune-modulating formulas: 4.5 to 14 g/L.
      - Fiber content: 5.9 to 24 g/L.
      - Osmolality: Isotonic ~300 mOsm/kg. Commercial range 280 to 875 mOsm/kg.
      - Renal solute load: 40 to 60 mL of water is the minimal amount necessary
        to excrete 1 g of nitrogen.

Formulary and Delivery System Considerations

  - Closed Systems: Ready-to-hang rigid plastic containers or bags. Hang
    time: 24 to 48 hours.
  - Open Systems: Cans, bottles, or reconstituted powder poured into a bag. Hang
    time: 4 to 8 hours.
  - Enteral Misconnections: Prevented by ISO-80369-3 compliant systems (ENFit).

  - Metabolic: Refeeding syndrome (hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia,
    hypomagnesemia, thiamine deficiency, sodium retention).
  - Gastrointestinal:
      - Gastric Residual Volume (GRV): Do not hold EN for GRV < 500 mL in the
        absence of other intolerance symptoms. If > 500 mL, decrease rate or
        discontinue.
      - Aspiration risk reduction: Elevate head of bed 30° to 45°, advance tube
        to small bowel, initiate prokinetics (metoclopramide, erythromycin).
        Blue food dye is contraindicated.
      - Diarrhea definition: >3 liquid stools daily or >250 to 500 mL/day for at
        least 2 consecutive days.
      - Diarrhea pharmacologic treatment: Loperamide, diphenoxylate/atropine,
        opioids.
  - Mechanical:
      - Flush with 15 to 30 mL water before and after medication.
      - Flush with 5 to 15 mL water between multiple medications.
      - Unclogging: Warm water or pancreatic enzymes mixed with sodium
        bicarbonate. Avoid carbonated beverages and cranberry juice.

Medication Delivery via Enteral Access Devices

  - Dosage Form Constraints: Do not crush sublingual,
    sustained/extended-release, or enteric-coated tablets.
  - Preparation: Crush appropriate solid dosage forms to fine powder and mix
    with 15 to 30 mL purified water.
  - Flushing: Pause EN, flush with at least 15 mL water before and after each
    individual medication.
